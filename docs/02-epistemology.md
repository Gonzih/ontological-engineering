# Epistemology

If ontology asks what exists in the company's knowledge system, epistemology asks how the company knows what it claims to know.

This distinction is essential. A company can have a well-structured ontology and still be epistemically weak. It can model concepts, decisions, users, agents, evidence, and specifications while failing to distinguish validated knowledge from speculation. In an AI-mediated organization, that failure becomes dangerous because agents can produce plausible language at high speed.

Epistemology is the discipline that protects the second brain from becoming a beautifully organized rumor system.

## Knowledge As Actionable Trust

Classical epistemology studies knowledge, belief, justification, evidence, skepticism, and error. For a company, these questions become operational:

- What do we believe?
- Why do we believe it?
- What evidence supports it?
- Who or what produced that evidence?
- What is the scope of the claim?
- How confident should we be?
- What would change our mind?
- Is this safe to use for product, strategy, code, or agent behavior?

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy frames epistemology around cognitive success and cognitive failure. For organizational design, that framing is useful because not every claim needs the same level of justification. The standard for a brainstorm is not the standard for a production migration. The standard for a design hypothesis is not the standard for a compliance requirement. The standard for an agent-generated summary is not the standard for a canonical company belief.

Knowledge in a company is not an abstract trophy. It is a permission to act.

## The Core Epistemic Objects

The second brain should distinguish several objects that are often blurred together:

- `Source`: where information came from.
- `Evidence`: material that supports, weakens, or contextualizes a claim.
- `Claim`: an evaluable proposition.
- `Assumption`: a claim temporarily accepted for planning or reasoning.
- `Hypothesis`: a claim being explored but not yet accepted.
- `Belief`: a claim the organization currently accepts within a scope.
- `Decision`: a commitment to action under goals, constraints, evidence, and assumptions.
- `Rationale`: the reasoning that connects evidence and assumptions to a decision.
- `Question`: an unresolved issue that should remain visible.
- `Disagreement`: a structured conflict between claims, interpretations, or priorities.

These objects form an epistemic chain. A source produces evidence. Evidence supports or challenges claims. Claims may become assumptions, hypotheses, or beliefs. Beliefs and assumptions inform decisions. Decisions generate requirements and specifications. Specifications generate software. Software produces feedback. Feedback becomes new evidence.

If the chain is preserved, the company can reason. If the chain is broken, the company only has artifacts.

## Provenance

Provenance is the memory of origin and transformation.

For human knowledge, provenance asks:

- Who said this?
- When was it said?
- In what context?
- Was it observed directly, reported secondhand, inferred, summarized, or interpreted?
- Was it challenged?
- Was it reviewed?
- Was it superseded?

For agentic knowledge, provenance adds more:

- Which agent produced this?
- Which model, tool, retrieval context, or prompt was used?
- Which inputs were visible?
- Which sources were cited or transformed?
- Was the output generated, extracted, synthesized, critiqued, or evaluated?
- Was a human involved before promotion?

Provenance is not clerical metadata. It is part of the claim's meaning. A statement from a customer interview, a metric dashboard, a generated synthesis, and a founder intuition may all be useful, but they should not be treated as the same kind of knowledge.

## Confidence Without False Precision

Organizations often want confidence scores. That desire is understandable but risky. A number can create the appearance of rigor without explaining why the system is confident.

A better model combines several dimensions:

- evidence quality: direct, indirect, anecdotal, synthetic, inferred
- source reliability: primary, secondary, internal, external, unknown
- recency: current, stale, historical, unknown
- scope: broad, narrow, experimental, context-specific
- review state: unreviewed, reviewed, validated, disputed
- decision impact: low, medium, high
- reversibility: easy to change, costly to change, irreversible

The goal is not to assign perfect certainty. The goal is to let humans and agents ask whether a claim is usable for a specific purpose.

An unreviewed agent synthesis may be acceptable for exploration but not for production behavior. A stale market claim may be acceptable as history but not as strategy. A customer anecdote may be valuable evidence but not proof of a general pattern.

## Social Epistemology

Companies are social knowledge systems. They do not know only through individuals. They know through meetings, documents, status rituals, authority structures, review processes, incentives, dashboards, naming conventions, and tools.

Social epistemology matters because organizational knowledge is shaped by power and process:

- Who is allowed to define terms?
- Whose observations become evidence?
- Which documents become canonical?
- Which disagreements are preserved?
- Which decisions require rationale?
- Which metrics are trusted?
- Which agent outputs are reviewed?
- Which teams can override previous beliefs?

The second brain should not pretend knowledge simply appears. It should represent the social processes that produce, validate, and promote knowledge.

This is especially important when AI agents enter the system. Agents can amplify existing biases, compress dissent into premature consensus, or overrepresent documents that are easy to retrieve. A socially aware epistemic model preserves minority evidence, unresolved disagreement, and context that might otherwise be summarized away.

## Disagreement And Ambiguity

A mature knowledge system does not erase disagreement too early.

Disagreement can be productive when it reveals:

- different interpretations of evidence
- conflicting user needs
- unresolved strategic priorities
- ambiguity in core concepts
- differences between short-term and long-term goals
- hidden assumptions in product or engineering decisions

Instead of forcing a single summary, the system should represent disagreement as an object. It should record what is disputed, who or what supports each side, what evidence is missing, and what decision depends on resolution.

Ambiguity should also be preserved when appropriate. Some concepts are still forming. Some terms should remain provisional. Premature formalization can make a young domain brittle.

## Epistemic Failure Modes

Common failures in agentic organizations include:

- treating generated text as verified knowledge
- treating retrieved text as relevant truth
- confusing source authority with evidence quality
- collapsing disagreement into a smooth synthesis
- losing the reason a decision was made
- using stale assumptions in new contexts
- preserving final decisions but not rejected alternatives
- confusing metrics with meaning
- allowing agents to act on claims that were only exploratory
- letting canonical language drift across teams
- updating docs without updating the ontology
- implementing requirements without preserving their evidential basis

These failures are not merely documentation problems. They are failures of organizational cognition.

## Epistemology As Governance

Epistemology becomes governance when it determines what agents and humans are allowed to do with knowledge.

Examples:

- A raw transcript can be searched but not treated as validated truth.
- A proposed claim can be discussed but not used in production policy.
- A validated decision can generate requirements.
- A deprecated belief can remain visible but must not guide new work.
- A high-risk specification requires review before code generation.
- An agent output must be promoted before other agents can rely on it.

This means epistemic status should be machine-readable. Agents should not have to infer from prose whether something is safe to use.

## Working Definition

Epistemology for ontological engineering is the discipline of representing how an organization forms, justifies, validates, disputes, revises, and acts on knowledge.

It gives the second brain standards for trust. Without it, ontology describes the shape of memory but not the legitimacy of what memory contains.
