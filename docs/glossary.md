# Glossary

This glossary defines working language for the repository. The definitions are intentionally oriented toward company knowledge systems rather than general dictionary use.

## Agent

An AI or software actor capable of performing tasks, producing outputs, using tools, reading memory, and sometimes modifying knowledge structures. In this repository, agents are epistemic actors because their outputs can affect what the organization later treats as knowledge.

## Agentic Knowledge Ecosystem

The governed network of humans, AI agents, tools, documents, schemas, workflows, and review processes that produces, validates, applies, and revises organizational knowledge.

## Artifact

Any produced object: code, document, design, prompt, test, dataset, report, simulation, or generated output. Artifacts should link back to the knowledge objects that produced them.

## Assumption

A claim accepted temporarily for reasoning, planning, design, or implementation. Assumptions require scope and revisit conditions because they can silently become conceptual debt.

## Belief

A claim the organization currently accepts as likely true or useful within a defined scope.

## Claim

An evaluable proposition. A claim may be supported, challenged, contradicted, accepted, disputed, deprecated, or superseded.

## Company Second Brain

A structured, temporal, relational memory system that preserves the organization's concepts, claims, evidence, decisions, rationale, specifications, artifacts, and learning history.

## Concept

A named idea or category with a definition, scope, boundary conditions, examples, counterexamples, relationships, and history.

## Conceptual Debt

The accumulated cost of unclear, outdated, contradictory, or unmodeled assumptions in a company's concepts, specifications, workflows, prompts, software, or agent instructions.

## Constraint

A limit that shapes possible action. Constraints may come from technical architecture, policy, risk, regulation, design principles, business strategy, or epistemic status.

## Decision

A commitment to action made under specific goals, evidence, assumptions, constraints, context, and tradeoffs.

## Disagreement

A structured conflict between claims, interpretations, priorities, or decisions. Disagreement should be preserved when resolving it prematurely would destroy useful context.

## Epistemology

The study and practice of how knowledge is formed, justified, trusted, disputed, revised, and acted upon.

## Evidence

Material that supports, weakens, contextualizes, or challenges a claim. Evidence should distinguish observation from interpretation.

## Feedback

Information produced by action in the world, such as user behavior, tests, incidents, metrics, reviews, or agent evaluations. Feedback can challenge upstream concepts, claims, assumptions, decisions, and specifications.

## Knowledge Engineering

The design of systems that represent knowledge in structured forms so humans and machines can reason with it.

## Ontology

A structured representation of the entities, concepts, relationships, constraints, and commitments in a domain.

## Ontological Commitment

A modeling choice about what distinctions matter. For example, treating a claim as different from a decision is an ontological commitment.

## Ontological Engineering

The practice of designing, maintaining, and evolving the conceptual structure through which an organization represents reality, coordinates humans and agents, preserves memory, and generates software.

## Provenance

The record of where knowledge came from, how it was produced, which transformations it went through, and how it changed.

## Rationale

The reasoning that connects evidence, assumptions, goals, constraints, alternatives, and tradeoffs to a decision.

## Relationship-Aware Knowledge

Knowledge represented through typed links such as supports, contradicts, supersedes, depends on, implements, constrains, and generates.

## Specification

A structured, versioned description of desired behavior, actors, concepts, interactions, requirements, constraints, assumptions, decisions, evidence, success criteria, and failure modes.

## Temporal Knowledge

Knowledge represented with time context, including when it was created, observed, valid, revised, superseded, deprecated, or reviewed.

## Wisdom

Structured judgment that preserves tradeoffs, context, limits, lessons, rejected alternatives, and reasons for action.
